Featured Exhibits

Explore some of the most iconic and innovative devices that shaped technology’s evolution.

Nintendo Entertainment System (1985)

The 8-bit icon that revitalized video gaming in the 1980s.

The Nintendo Entertainment System (NES), released in 1985 and discontinued in 1995, revived the video game industry after the 1983 crash. It introduced iconic franchises like Super Mario Bros. and Zelda, sold over 60 million units, and featured the first widespread use of the D-pad.

Sony Walkman (1979)

A revolution in portable music — freedom in your pocket.

Launched in 1979, the original cassette-based Walkman redefined personal music. It sold over 200 million units before cassette models were discontinued in 2010. It created the “Walkman effect” — music as a private, mobile experience.

Philips LaserDisc Player (1978)

Cinematic tech ahead of its time, paving the way for DVDs.

Debuting in 1978 and discontinued by 2001, LaserDisc offered superior video and audio quality but lacked recording capability. Though never mainstream, it paved the way for CD and DVD technology and was beloved by film collectors.

Apple IIe (1983)

A key player in making home computing mainstream in the '80s.

Released in 1983 and retired in 1993, the Apple IIe was Apple’s longest-running model. It brought 80-column text, lowercase support, and expandability, making it a staple in schools and homes during the early personal computing boom.

iPhone (1st Gen) (2007)

The touchscreen revolution that defined the modern smartphone.

Unveiled in 2007 and discontinued in 2008, the first iPhone combined phone, iPod, and internet into one sleek device. It introduced multi-touch and visual voicemail, launching the smartphone era and reshaping mobile technology forever.

Napster (1999)

The digital disruptor that changed how we share and discover music.

Launched in 1999 and shut down in 2001, Napster revolutionized music sharing with peer-to-peer MP3 downloads. It sparked legal battles and reshaped the music industry, paving the way for streaming platforms like Spotify and Apple Music.

Motorola Bag Phone (1988)

The bold, brick-sized gateway to mobile communication.

Introduced in 1988 and produced through 2000, the Motorola Bag Phone offered powerful 3-watt analog signal strength, making it ideal for rural and mobile users. Its rugged design made it a favorite among truckers and emergency responders.
